# Article Structure — Specification

This document defines the structural pattern underlying all articles.

This is not a rigid template.

It is a **conceptual spine** that ensures:

* clarity
* progression
* coherence
* alignment with the thesis

Structure should guide the writing — not constrain it.

---

## 1. Core Structural Pattern

All articles follow a recurring pattern:

```plaintext
Intuition → Compression → Expansion → Reframe → Integration → Reflection
```

This is the natural movement of the writing.

---

## 2. Structural Phases

### Phase 1 — Entry (Intuition)

Purpose:

* ground the reader
* establish relevance
* create curiosity

Two valid modes:

#### A. Personal Entry

* lived experience
* internal observation
* emotional or cognitive shift

#### B. Conceptual Entry

* external observation
* system-level statement
* generalizable pattern

Constraint:

* must lead naturally into the core idea
* must not feel abstract without grounding

---

### Phase 2 — Core Compression

Purpose:

* state the idea clearly
* reduce ambiguity

This is where the reader should understand:

* what the article is about
* what it is pointing toward

Constraints:

* avoid jargon
* avoid over-explaining
* one clear direction

---

### Phase 3 — Expansion

Purpose:

* unpack the idea
* explore implications
* build understanding progressively

Characteristics:

* layered sections
* each section introduces one core idea
* transitions feel necessary, not forced

Constraints:

* no conceptual branching without relevance
* avoid introducing unrelated domains

---

### Phase 4 — Structural Clarification (Visual Integration)

Purpose:

* compress complexity through visuals
* clarify relationships that are difficult to express linearly

Visuals typically appear:

* after conceptual buildup
* at points of potential confusion
* when relationships become multi-dimensional

Constraints:

* visuals must not introduce new ideas
* visuals must reinforce existing structure

---

### Phase 5 — Reframe / Pivot

Purpose:

* shift perspective
* introduce deeper or alternative interpretation

Common patterns:

* “There is another way to see this…”
* “The deeper issue is…”
* “The problem is not…”

Constraints:

* must build on previous sections
* must not contradict without explanation

---

### Phase 6 — Integration

Purpose:

* connect ideas into a coherent whole
* translate concept into application or stance

This may include:

* practical implications
* cognitive reframing
* system-level insight

Constraints:

* avoid prescriptive tone
* avoid forced conclusions

---

### Phase 7 — Closing Reflection

Purpose:

* compress the article into a final insight
* leave the reader with something to carry forward

Characteristics:

* calm tone
* non-dogmatic
* open-ended but grounded

Avoid:

* summaries
* repetition
* exaggerated conclusions

---

## 3. Section Design Rules

Each section should:

* introduce one primary idea
* build on previous context
* move the reader forward

Avoid:

* multi-idea sections
* abrupt transitions
* redundant explanations

---

## 4. Compression Moments (Quote Blocks)

Certain ideas benefit from being visually separated.

Use quote blocks when:

* a core principle is being distilled
* a transition point needs emphasis
* the article reaches a conceptual turning point
* the closing insight needs to land cleanly

Example pattern:

```plaintext
> This is the distilled insight.
```

Constraints:

* use sparingly (2–4 per article is typical)
* do not use for simple emphasis
* do not over-stack multiple quote blocks
* each should feel like a “pause,” not decoration

---

## 5. Table of Contents Placement

If included, the Table of Contents should:

* appear immediately after the header image and caption
* mirror section headers exactly
* use plain text (no links or anchors)

Purpose:

* improve navigation
* reinforce structure
* not interrupt flow

---

## 6. Flow Integrity

A well-structured article should feel:

* inevitable (each section follows naturally)
* progressive (understanding builds over time)
* coherent (no conceptual jumps)

If a section can be removed without loss:

* it should be removed

---

## 7. Structural Anti-Patterns

Do not:

* front-load all complexity
* jump between unrelated ideas
* overuse abstraction without grounding
* introduce ideas late without development
* force resolution where uncertainty is appropriate

---

## 8. Relationship to Template

The template defines:

* formatting
* layout
* placement of visuals

This specification defines:

* movement
* progression
* conceptual integrity

The template may change.

This structure should remain stable.

---

## 9. Guiding Constraint

If the reader cannot answer:

* What is the idea?
* How did we get here?
* Why does it matter?

Then the structure has failed.

Structure is not visible when done well.

It is felt.
