# Research & Grounding — Specification

Articles make claims.

Claims require grounding.

Grounding is not decoration. It is **structural support for the idea being presented**.

---

## 1. Core Principle

Research exists to:

* strengthen the thesis
* clarify mechanisms
* increase trust

It does NOT exist to:

* signal intelligence
* overwhelm the reader
* replace original thinking

---

## 2. Minimum Requirements

Each article must:

* include **3–6 credible sources**
* include **at least one high-quality (Tier 1 or Tier 2) source**
* avoid unsupported claims when making factual assertions
* avoid vague phrases like “studies show”

Research must map to **structural pillars**, not appear randomly.

---

## 3. Source Hierarchy

Sources are prioritized as follows:

| Tier | Type                                    |
| ---- | --------------------------------------- |
| 1    | Peer-reviewed research                  |
| 2    | Foundational frameworks / seminal texts |
| 3    | Recognized domain experts               |
| 4    | Credible long-form journalism           |
| 5    | Primary documentation                   |

Prefer higher tiers when available.

Lower-tier sources may be used when:

* they are directly relevant
* they support lived experience
* they clarify real-world application

---

## 4. Integration Rules

Research must be:

* introduced naturally
* clearly connected to the idea
* easy to understand

Avoid:

* dropping citations without explanation
* overloading sections with references
* clustering multiple sources unnecessarily

Each reference should answer:

> Why is this here?

---

## 5. Citation Strategy

### When to Cite

Cite when:

* making factual claims
* referencing theories or frameworks
* introducing unfamiliar concepts
* supporting non-obvious assertions

Do NOT cite:

* obvious observations
* personal experiences
* widely understood concepts

---

### How to Cite (In-Text)

Use natural integration:

* “Cognitive Load Theory suggests…”
* “Research on uncertainty tolerance shows…”

Avoid:

* academic citation clutter inside paragraphs
* excessive parentheses

---

## 6. Reference Section Requirements

Each article must include a **References** section.

Each reference should include:

* Author
* Year
* Title
* Source (journal, book, etc.)
* DOI or link (preferred)

Consistency is more important than format perfection.

---

## 7. Epistemic Discipline

All research usage must respect:

### Accuracy

* Do not misrepresent findings
* Do not exaggerate conclusions

### Scope Awareness

* Do not generalize beyond study conditions
* Note when findings are context-dependent

### Causality vs Correlation

* Do not imply causation unless established
* Avoid oversimplification

---

## 8. Avoiding Research Misuse

Do NOT:

* cherry-pick studies to support a narrative
* use research as authority without understanding
* import findings across unrelated domains without explanation
* rely on AI-generated summaries without verification

---

## 9. Relationship to Voice

Research should:

* support the writing
* not dominate it

The article should still feel like:

* a human perspective
* not a literature review

---

## 10. Optional Research Notes

Research notes may be stored in:

```plaintext
articles/YYYY-MM-slug/assets/research-notes.md
```

This file is:

* optional
* not published
* used for deeper exploration

---

## 11. Medium Alignment

Medium prioritizes:

* clarity
* credibility
* reader value

Overuse of research can:

* reduce readability
* weaken narrative flow

Underuse of research can:

* reduce trust
* weaken claims

Balance is required.

---

## 12. Guiding Constraint

If research:

* distracts from the idea
* overwhelms the reader
* or replaces original thinking

It should be reduced.

If a claim:

* feels unsupported
* or overly confident

It should be grounded.

The goal is not to prove.

The goal is to **support understanding**.
