# Voice & Style — Specification

This document defines the writing voice.

It exists to preserve:

* authenticity
* clarity
* conceptual precision

The goal is not to standardize expression.

The goal is to **prevent drift into generic, artificial, or performative writing**.

---

## 1. Core Voice Characteristics

The writing voice is:

* reflective
* calm
* precise
* grounded
* non-performative

It should feel like:

* thinking in real time
* ideas being clarified, not presented as final truth
* insight emerging, not being declared

---

## 2. Primary Writing Pattern

All writing follows a natural rhythm:

```plaintext
simple → precise → expanded → simplified
```

This creates:

* accessibility at entry
* depth during exploration
* clarity at exit

---

## 3. Sentence Design

### Preferred

* short to medium-length sentences
* clear phrasing
* direct statements

Example pattern:

* statement
* clarification
* expansion

---

### Avoid

* overly long, complex sentences
* unnecessary qualifiers
* inflated language

---

## 4. Paragraph Structure

Paragraphs should:

* focus on one idea
* use spacing to create rhythm
* avoid density

Common pattern:

* 1–2 sentence paragraph
* followed by expansion
* followed by transition

White space is intentional.

---

## 5. Tone Constraints

The tone must:

* avoid certainty when not justified
* avoid ideological framing
* avoid emotional manipulation

Do not:

* preach
* persuade aggressively
* moralize

Instead:

* present
* explore
* reveal

---

## 6. Language Rules

### Use

* concrete language when possible
* simple phrasing for complex ideas
* metaphor only when clarifying

### Avoid Use Of

* buzzwords
* vague abstractions without grounding
* filler phrases

Examples to avoid:

* “in today’s world”
* “it is important to note”
* “this highlights the fact that”

---

## 7. Concept Handling

When introducing ideas:

* start from intuition
* define clearly
* expand gradually

Avoid:

* dropping complex concepts without setup
* assuming prior knowledge
* stacking abstractions

---

## 8. Emotional Layer

Emotion is allowed, but:

* it must be grounded
* it must not overwhelm structure

Personal writing should feel:

* honest
* controlled
* integrated

Not:

* chaotic
* venting
* performative vulnerability

---

## 9. Authority and Humility

The voice should reflect:

* confidence in reasoning
* openness to revision

Avoid:

* absolute claims
* dismissive tone
* intellectual superiority

Preferred stance:

“I may be wrong, but this is what the structure suggests.”

---

## 10. Transitions

Transitions should feel:

* natural
* necessary
* invisible

Common transition types:

* extension (“This leads to…”)
* contrast (“But something else is happening…”)
* reframe (“A different way to see this…”)

Avoid:

* abrupt jumps
* forced connections

---

## 11. Ending Style

Closings should:

* compress the idea
* remain calm
* avoid finality

Avoid:

* summaries
* dramatic conclusions
* overstatement

The ending should feel like:

* a continuation of thought
* not a conclusion of argument

---

## 12. Anti-Patterns (Critical)

Avoid writing that feels:

### Generic

* interchangeable with other articles
* lacking personal signal

### Over-Polished

* too smooth
* too symmetrical
* emotionally flat

### AI-Like

* repetitive phrasing
* predictable structure
* excessive clarity without depth

### Performative

* written to impress rather than express
* exaggerated complexity

---

## 13. Alignment with Medium

Medium prioritizes:

* authentic voice
* original thinking
* reader value

Writing that feels:

* human
* grounded
* intentional

is more likely to receive distribution.

---

## 14. Guiding Constraint

If the writing feels like:

* it could have been written by anyone
* or generated without lived experience

it should be revised.

The goal is not perfection.

The goal is **signal**.
